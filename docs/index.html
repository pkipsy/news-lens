NewsLens v 0.1

Here you can take a "deep dive" into the text data behind the NewsLens classifier. To browse the interactive visualizations, simply scroll down and click links of interest.

This visualization uses the open source Python library, Scattertext, to illustrate differences between left and right-leaning media. It identifies key topics that are characteristic of both sides, as well as topics that are used more frequently by one side or the other. These differences are visible for both very common words (like Donald Trump) and rarer words (like CBO).

The data for this visualization is drawn from two-years of headlines scraped from the bipartisan organization All Sides Media. All Sides looks to provide a more balanced approach to news coverage by rounding up the top headlines of the day and showcasing reporting of these stories by outlets on the left, right, and center.

The headline crawl yielded more than 3,000 articles, from over 100 of the most influential media organizations in the US today, including the BBC, Fox News, and the New York Times.

You can open this visualization in a new tab by clicking here.

This next set of visualizations relies on the open source Python library, PyLDAviz, for interactive topic model visualization.

Separate models and visualizations have been created for left, right, and center-leaning media articles, from the All Sides headline crawl.

Each visualization makes it possible to explore the latent topics in these texts, along with the words that are most closely associated with them. The topics are ordered by their prominence in the texts.

There is a lot to uncover! The Russia investigation, school shootings, terrorism and climate change, are just a few of the topics that vary in their coverage across party lines.

Want to take a closer look at the media outlets in our dataset? This interactive visualization made with plotly shows their distribution across the political spectrum. Each bubble corresponds to a different news outlet.

With this time series visualization, you can inspect how differently biased outlets were sampled over the time-span of the crawl.

This simple chart shows 25 of the most informative words for liberal and conservative texts. Nearly half are news boilerplate. Identifying and removing boilerplate is thus essential for building an "honest" classifier.

Without this preprocessing step, the idiosyncracies of various news sites "tip off" the classifier. The likely consequence? The classifier will be less successful at generalizing to novel sources.

Finally, this joy plot, made with seaborn, shows the distribution of sentiment across these articles, an analysis made possible by Google's Natural Language REST API.

Clearly, there is not much difference in aggregate. More fine-grained analyses, such as looking at sentiment in relation to specific topics (like immigration or the economy), are required to construct useful features for classification.
